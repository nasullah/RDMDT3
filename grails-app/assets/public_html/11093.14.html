  • Inclusion Criteria

    - Patients with focal epilepsy not thought to be acquired in aetiology
    - At least two first degree relatives or affected family members across three generations who have focal epilepsy that is not thought to be acquired in aetiology
    - All forms of focal epilepsy are permissible

    Exclusion Criteria

    - Suspected acquired cause of epilepsy
    - EEG demonstrates definite generalised epileptiform discharges

    Prior Genetic Testing

    - Results should have been reviewed for all genetic tests undertaken, including disease-relevant genes in exome sequencing data. The patient is not eligible if they have a molecular diagnosis for their condition.
    - Genetic testing should continue according to routine local practice for this phenotype regardless of recruitment to the project; results of these tests must be submitted via the ‘Genetic investigations’ section of the data capture tool to allow comparison of WGS with current standard testing.

    PLEASE NOTE: The sensitivity of WGS compared to current diagnostic genetic testing has not yet been established. It is therefore important that tests which are clinically indicated under local standard practice continue to be carried out.

  • Febrile seizures

    Febrile seizures are convulsions induced by a fever in infants or small children and are generally characterized by loss of consciousness and tonic-clonic movements. Most febrile seizures last a minute or two.

    Focal seizures without impairment of consciousness or awareness

    A partial seizure in which consciousness is maintained.

    Focal seizures with impairment of consciousness or awareness

    A partial seizure characterized by impairment or loss of consciousness.

    Auditory auras

    Auras with sensations of buzzing, drumming sounds or single tones.

    Epigastric auras

    Auras with abdominal discomfort including nausea, emptiness, tightness, churning, butterflies, malaise, pain, and hunger; sensation may rise to chest or throat. Some phenomena may reflect ictal autonomic dysfunction.

    Psychic auras

    Auras with affective, mnemonic or composite perceptual phenomena including illusory or composite hallucinatory events.

    Comment:
    Events may appear alone or in combination. Included are feelings of depersonalisation. These phenomena have subjective qualities similar to those experienced in life but are recognised by the subject as occurring outside of actual context.

    Vegetative auras

    A sensation consistent with involvement of the autonomic nervous system, including cardiovascular, gastrointestinal, sudomotor, vasomotor and thermoregulatory functions.

    Gustatory auras

    Auras with taste sensations including acidic, bitter, salty , sweet or metallic tastes.

    Olfactory Auras

    Auras with sensation of odor, usually disagreeable.

    Somatosensory auras

    Auras with sensation of tingling, numbness, electric shock sensations, pain, sense of movement, or desire to move.

    Visual auras

    Auras with sensation of flashing or flickering lights, spots, simple patterns, scotomata, or amaurosis.

    Comment:
    Visual auras result from partial seizures affecting an occipital lobe in which consciousness is maintained.

    Bilateral convulsive seizures

    Bilateral convulsive seizures are focal seizure with secondary bilateral motor phenomena, not primary generalized seizures.

    EEG abnormality

    Abnormality observed by electroencephalogram (EEG), which is used to record of the brain's spontaneous electrical activity from multiple electrodes placed on the scalp.

    EEG with focal spike waves

    EEG with focal sharp transient waves of a duration less than 80 msec followed by a slow wave.

    EEG with focal sharp waves

    EEG with focal sharp transient waves of a duration between 80 and 200 msec.

    EEG with focal sharp slow waves

    EEG with focal sharp transient waves of a duration between 80 and 200 msec followed by a slow wave.

    EEG with focal spikes

    EEG with focal sharp transient waves of a duration less than 80 msec.

    EEG with frontal focal spike waves

    EEG with focal sharp transient waves in the frontal region, i.e., focal sharp waves of a duration less than 80 msec followed by a slow wave.

    EEG with frontal focal spikes

    EEG with focal sharp transient waves of a duration less than 80 msec in the frontal region.

    EEG with frontal sharp slow waves

    EEG with sharp slow waves in the frontal region. Sharp slow waves are focal sharp transient waves of a duration between 80 and 200 msec followed by a slow wave.

    EEG with frontal sharp waves

    EEG with sharp waves in the frontal region, i.e., sharp transient waves of a duration between 80 and 200 msec.

    EEG with occipital focal spike waves

    EEG with focal sharp transient waves in the occipital region, i.e., focal sharp waves of a duration less than 80 msec followed by a slow wave.

    EEG with occipital focal spikes

    EEG with focal sharp transient waves of a duration less than 80 msec in the occipital region.

    EEG with occipital sharp slow waves

    EEG with sharp slow waves in the occipital region. Sharp slow waves are focal sharp transient waves of a duration between 80 and 200 msec followed by a slow wave.

    EEG with occipital sharp waves

    EEG with sharp waves in the parietal region, i.e., sharp transient waves of a duration between 80 and 200 msec.

    EEG with temporal focal spike waves

    EEG with focal sharp transient waves in the parietal region, i.e., focal sharp waves of a duration less than 80 msec followed by a slow wave.

    EEG with temporal focal spikes

    EEG with focal sharp transient waves of a duration less than 80 msec in the temporal region.

    EEG with temporal sharp slow waves

    EEG with sharp slow waves in the temporal region. Sharp slow waves are focal sharp transient waves of a duration between 80 and 200 msec followed by a slow wave.

    EEG with temporal sharp waves

    EEG with sharp waves in the temporal region, i.e., sharp transient waves of a duration between 80 and 200 msec.

    EEG with parietal focal spike waves

    EEG with focal sharp transient waves in the parietal region, i.e., focal sharp waves of a duration less than 80 msec followed by a slow wave.

    EEG with parietal focal spikes

    EEG with focal sharp transient waves of a duration less than 80 msec in the parietal region.

    EEG with parietal sharp slow waves

    EEG with sharp slow waves in the parietal region. Sharp slow waves are focal sharp transient waves of a duration between 80 and 200 msec followed by a slow wave.

    EEG with parietal sharp waves

    EEG with sharp waves in the parietal region, i.e., sharp transient waves of a duration between 80 and 200 msec.

    EEG with centrotemporal focal spike waves

    EEG with focal sharp transient waves in the centrotemporal region of the brain (also known as the central sulcus), i.e., focal sharp waves of a duration less than 80 msec followed by a slow wave.

    EEG with central focal spike waves

    EEG with focal sharp transient waves in the central region, i.e., focal sharp waves of a duration less than 80 msec followed by a slow wave.

    EEG with central focal spikes

    EEG with focal sharp transient waves of a duration less than 80 msec in the central region.

    EEG with central sharp slow waves

    EEG with sharp slow waves in the central region. Sharp slow waves are focal sharp transient waves of a duration between 80 and 200 msec followed by a slow wave.

    EEG with central sharp waves

    EEG with sharp waves in the central region, i.e., sharp transient waves of a duration between 80 and 200 msec.

    EEG with multifocal slow activity

    Multifocal slowing of cerebral electrical activity recorded along the scalp by electroencephalography (EEG).
